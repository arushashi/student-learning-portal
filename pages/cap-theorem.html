CAP Theorem

Fundamental trade-offs in distributed database systems

Definition

What is the CAP Theorem?

CAP theorem states that a distributed system can only provide two of three guarantees: Consistency, Availability, and Partition Tolerance. Also known as Brewer's theorem (proposed by Eric Brewer in 2000), it is a fundamental principle guiding the design of distributed databases and systems.

Key Insight: In the presence of a network partition, a distributed system must choose between consistency and availability. You cannot have all three guarantees simultaneously when partitions occur.

Simple Explanation

Understanding the Concept

Imagine you and your friend each have a copy of a shared notebook. When you write something new, your friend's copy should also update. But what if the phone line between you goes down (a partition)? You have two choices: stop writing until the line is restored (sacrifice availability for consistency) or keep writing knowing your copies may diverge (sacrifice consistency for availability).

The Three Properties

1. Consistency (C)

  • All nodes see the same data at the same time
  • Every read receives the most recent write or an error
  • All clients have an identical view of the data

2. Availability (A)

  • Every request receives a (non-error) response
  • The system remains operational at all times
  • No guarantee the response contains the most recent data

3. Partition Tolerance (P)

  • System continues operating despite network partitions
  • Messages between nodes can be lost or delayed
  • Essential for any distributed system in production

The Trade-offs

CP (Consistency + Partition Tolerance): Sacrifice availability. The system may refuse requests during a partition to maintain consistency. Examples: MongoDB, HBase, Redis.

AP (Availability + Partition Tolerance): Sacrifice consistency. The system remains available but may return stale data. Examples: Cassandra, DynamoDB, CouchDB.

CA (Consistency + Availability): No partition tolerance. Only possible when there are no network partitions, essentially a single-node system. Examples: Traditional RDBMS (PostgreSQL, MySQL on a single node).

Real-World Example

Amazon's DynamoDB (AP System)

Amazon designed DynamoDB to prioritize availability over consistency. During the holiday shopping season, it's more important that customers can always add items to their cart (availability) even if there's a brief inconsistency across data centers. Amazon uses eventual consistency to reconcile data after partitions heal.

Key Points & Summary

Consistency Models

Strong consistency ensures all nodes see the same data simultaneously, while eventual consistency allows temporary divergence that converges over time.

Partition Reality

Network partitions are inevitable in distributed systems. The real choice is between consistency and availability when partitions occur.

Trade-off Spectrum

CAP is not binary. Systems can tune their consistency and availability guarantees on a spectrum depending on the operation or configuration.

PACELC Extension

The PACELC theorem extends CAP: even without partitions (Else), systems must choose between latency and consistency, adding another dimension.

Quick Summary

  • CAP theorem defines trade-offs in distributed systems
  • You can only guarantee two of three: Consistency, Availability, Partition Tolerance
  • Network partitions are unavoidable, so the real choice is C vs A
  • CP systems sacrifice availability for data accuracy
  • AP systems sacrifice consistency for uptime

Interview Questions & Answers

What is the CAP theorem and why is it important?

The CAP theorem states that a distributed data store can only provide two out of three guarantees simultaneously:

  • Consistency: All nodes see the same data at the same time
  • Availability: Every request receives a response (success or failure)
  • Partition Tolerance: The system continues to operate despite network partitions

It's important because it guides architects in making fundamental design decisions about distributed systems based on their requirements.

Can you give examples of CP and AP systems?

CP systems prioritize consistency over availability:

  • MongoDB: Uses replica sets with primary-based writes for strong consistency
  • HBase: Built on HDFS, provides strong consistency guarantees
  • Redis (cluster mode): Rejects writes during partitions to maintain consistency

AP systems prioritize availability over consistency:

  • Cassandra: Uses eventual consistency with tunable consistency levels
  • DynamoDB: Designed for high availability with eventual consistency
  • CouchDB: Multi-master replication with eventual consistency

What is eventual consistency and how does it relate to CAP?

Eventual consistency is a consistency model where:

  • Temporary Divergence: Replicas may temporarily have different values
  • Convergence: Given enough time without new updates, all replicas converge to the same value
  • Trade-off: It's the consistency model chosen by AP systems to maintain availability
  • Use Cases: Social media feeds, DNS, shopping carts where slight staleness is acceptable

Why can't we have all three properties of CAP?

When a network partition occurs, a distributed system faces an impossible choice:

  • If you choose consistency: You must block or reject requests to nodes that can't communicate, sacrificing availability
  • If you choose availability: You must serve requests even with stale data, sacrificing consistency
  • Partition tolerance is mandatory: In real-world distributed systems, network failures will occur, so partition tolerance cannot be sacrificed

This makes the real-world choice between CP and AP, since network partitions are inevitable in production systems.
