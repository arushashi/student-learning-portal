Distributed Transactions

Coordinate transactions across multiple services and databases using 2PC, 3PC, and Saga patterns to maintain data consistency

Definition

What are Distributed Transactions?

A distributed transaction is a database transaction that spans multiple networked resources — such as different databases, microservices, or message queues — and must either fully complete (commit) across all participants or fully roll back, maintaining atomicity. Unlike local transactions that operate within a single database, distributed transactions must coordinate multiple independent systems that can fail independently, making consistency guarantees significantly more complex. Key approaches include Two-Phase Commit (2PC), Three-Phase Commit (3PC), and the Saga pattern.

Key Insight: Distributed transactions represent a fundamental trade-off in system design. ACID transactions (2PC/3PC) provide strong consistency but reduce availability and performance, while BASE approaches (Sagas) provide eventual consistency with better availability — reflecting the CAP theorem constraints.

Simple Explanation

Understanding the Concept

Imagine booking a vacation that requires a flight, a hotel, and a rental car. You only want the trip if you can book all three. If the hotel is full, you need to cancel the flight and car reservations too. A distributed transaction is the system that coordinates all these separate bookings so they either all succeed together or all get cancelled — no one ends up with just a flight and no hotel.

In distributed systems, there are three primary approaches to handling this coordination:

  • Two-Phase Commit (2PC): A coordinator asks all participants to prepare (Phase 1: vote yes/no). If all vote yes, the coordinator tells everyone to commit (Phase 2). If any votes no, all abort. This provides strong atomicity but blocks if the coordinator fails after Phase 1.
  • Three-Phase Commit (3PC): Adds a pre-commit phase between prepare and commit to reduce blocking. If the coordinator crashes, participants can determine the outcome based on the pre-commit state. However, it still cannot handle network partitions perfectly.
  • Saga Pattern: Breaks a long transaction into a sequence of local transactions, each with a compensating action. If step 3 fails, compensating transactions undo steps 2 and 1. Sagas sacrifice atomicity for availability and work well in microservices architectures.
  • ACID vs BASE: 2PC provides ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability) guarantees. Sagas follow BASE (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventually consistent) semantics — the system may be temporarily inconsistent but will converge.

Real-World Example

Payment Processing Systems

When you purchase something online, the payment flow involves multiple services: the order service creates the order, the payment service charges your card, the inventory service reserves stock, and the notification service sends a confirmation email. These must be coordinated as a distributed transaction. Companies like Stripe and PayPal use Saga-based approaches internally: if the payment succeeds but inventory reservation fails, a compensating transaction refunds the payment. The system eventually reaches a consistent state where either the full order is processed or all partial changes are rolled back.

Key Points & Summary

Two-Phase Commit (2PC)

The coordinator sends a prepare request to all participants. Each participant votes yes (prepared) or no (abort). If all vote yes, the coordinator sends commit; otherwise, abort. The protocol is blocking: if the coordinator crashes after prepare but before commit, participants are stuck holding locks until the coordinator recovers.

Saga Pattern

A Saga is a sequence of local transactions where each step has a compensating action. Two orchestration strategies exist: Choreography (events trigger the next step) and Orchestration (a central coordinator directs steps). Sagas provide eventual consistency without distributed locks, making them ideal for microservices.

ACID vs BASE

ACID ensures strict consistency through distributed locking (2PC), suitable for financial systems. BASE accepts temporary inconsistency for higher availability and performance, suitable for e-commerce and social media. Most modern microservices architectures lean toward BASE semantics with Sagas.

Failure Handling

Distributed transactions must handle coordinator failures, participant failures, network timeouts, and partial commits. Strategies include timeout-based abort, transaction logs for recovery, idempotent operations to safely retry, and compensating transactions to undo partial work.

Quick Summary

  • Distributed transactions coordinate operations across multiple services/databases
  • 2PC provides strong atomicity but is blocking and reduces availability
  • 3PC reduces blocking but adds complexity and extra network round-trips
  • Sagas break long transactions into compensatable local transactions
  • ACID guarantees consistency; BASE accepts eventual consistency for availability
  • Modern microservices favor Sagas over 2PC for better fault tolerance

Interview Questions & Answers

What is the Two-Phase Commit protocol and what are its limitations?

Two-Phase Commit (2PC) is a protocol that ensures atomic commitment across multiple databases:

  • Phase 1 (Prepare): The coordinator asks all participants if they can commit. Each participant validates the transaction, acquires locks, writes to its WAL (write-ahead log), and responds yes or no.
  • Phase 2 (Commit/Abort): If all participants voted yes, the coordinator sends commit. If any voted no, it sends abort. Participants then finalize accordingly.
  • Blocking Problem: If the coordinator crashes after Phase 1, participants that voted yes are stuck holding locks indefinitely, unable to commit or abort until the coordinator recovers.
  • Performance: 2PC requires at least 2 round-trips and holds distributed locks for the entire duration, significantly reducing throughput.
  • Single Point of Failure: The coordinator is a single point of failure. If it crashes with the commit decision, the entire distributed transaction is blocked.

How does the Saga pattern differ from 2PC, and when would you choose each?

Sagas and 2PC represent fundamentally different approaches to distributed transactions:

  • Isolation: 2PC provides full isolation (ACID) — intermediate states are never visible. Sagas allow intermediate states to be visible since each local transaction commits independently.
  • Availability: 2PC blocks on coordinator/participant failures. Sagas are non-blocking; each step operates independently and compensating transactions handle failures asynchronously.
  • Choose 2PC when: You need strict atomicity across databases (e.g., financial transfers between banks), and latency/availability trade-offs are acceptable.
  • Choose Sagas when: You have microservices with separate databases, need high availability, and can tolerate brief inconsistency (e.g., e-commerce order processing, travel booking).
  • Saga Challenges: Designing compensating transactions is complex. You must handle idempotency, handle concurrent sagas affecting the same data, and deal with the "semantic rollback" problem where some actions cannot be truly undone (e.g., a sent email).

Explain Saga orchestration vs choreography.

There are two ways to coordinate saga steps:

  • Orchestration: A central saga orchestrator (coordinator service) tells each participant what to do and when. The orchestrator maintains the saga's state and decides on compensating actions. Easier to understand and debug, but the orchestrator is a potential bottleneck and single point of failure.
  • Choreography: Each service listens for events and decides independently what to do next. The order service emits an "OrderCreated" event; the payment service hears it and processes payment, emitting "PaymentProcessed"; the inventory service hears that and reserves stock. No central coordinator, but the flow is harder to follow and debug.
  • Orchestration Advantages: Clear workflow visibility, easier error handling, simpler to add new steps.
  • Choreography Advantages: No single point of failure, looser coupling, better for simple workflows with few steps.
  • Recommendation: Use orchestration for complex sagas with many steps. Use choreography for simple, few-step flows where coupling is a concern.

What is the difference between ACID and BASE, and how does it relate to distributed transactions?

ACID and BASE represent two philosophies for transaction guarantees in distributed systems:

  • ACID: Atomicity (all or nothing), Consistency (valid state transitions), Isolation (transactions don't see each other's intermediate states), Durability (committed data survives crashes). Achieved through 2PC/3PC with distributed locks.
  • BASE: Basically Available (system remains operational), Soft state (state may change over time without input), Eventually consistent (system will converge to consistent state). Achieved through Sagas, event sourcing, and async messaging.
  • Trade-off: ACID sacrifices availability and performance for consistency. BASE sacrifices immediate consistency for availability and partition tolerance — aligned with the CAP theorem.
  • Practical Choice: Banking transfers need ACID (you can't have money appear in two accounts or disappear). E-commerce inventory can use BASE (briefly overselling then compensating is acceptable).
