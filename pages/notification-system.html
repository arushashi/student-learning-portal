Notification System Design

Designing a scalable notification system supporting push, SMS, and email channels

Definition

What is a Notification System?

A notification system is a platform that sends timely, relevant alerts to users across multiple delivery channels including push notifications (iOS/Android), SMS, email, in-app messages, and webhooks. It acts as a centralized hub that receives notification requests from various services, applies user preferences and rate limits, renders messages from templates, routes them through the appropriate delivery channel, and tracks delivery status. At scale, systems like Facebook's notification platform handle billions of notifications per day across billions of users, requiring sophisticated queue management, prioritization, and fault-tolerant delivery pipelines.

Key Insight: A great notification system is invisible when working correctly but immediately noticed when it fails. The challenge isn't just sending messages — it's sending the right message, to the right user, at the right time, through the right channel, without overwhelming them with notification fatigue.

Simple Explanation

Understanding the Concept

Think of a notification system like a sophisticated mail room in a large corporate building. Departments (services) drop off messages to the mail room. The mail room staff (notification service) checks each recipient's preferences: do they want physical mail, email, or phone calls? They prioritize urgent messages over routine ones, batch similar messages together to avoid overwhelming recipients, and keep a log of every delivery attempt. If a delivery fails, they retry with a different method.

The key complexity is that this mail room handles millions of messages per minute, each with different priorities, channels, and recipient preferences. The system must be both fast (urgent alerts within seconds) and respectful (not sending marketing emails at 3 AM). It must also be extremely reliable — a missed security alert or password reset email directly impacts user trust.

  • Priority Queues: Notifications are classified into priority tiers (critical, high, medium, low). Security alerts and OTP codes are processed immediately from the critical queue, while marketing emails wait in the low-priority queue. Separate queues per priority prevent low-priority floods from delaying critical notifications
  • Rate Limiting: Per-user and per-channel rate limits prevent notification fatigue. A user might receive at most 5 push notifications per hour, 3 SMS per day, and 10 emails per day. Global rate limits protect downstream providers (e.g., SMS gateway limits) from being overwhelmed
  • Template Engine: Notifications are rendered from templates with variable interpolation, supporting localization (i18n) and personalization. A single template like "Hi {{name}}, your order {{orderId}} has shipped" serves millions of users in their preferred language
  • Delivery Tracking: Each notification is tracked through its lifecycle: queued, sent, delivered, opened, clicked, or failed. This data feeds dashboards for delivery rates, open rates, and channel performance, and triggers automatic retries on failure

Real-World Example

Facebook Notification System

Facebook's notification system serves over 2 billion users across push, email, SMS, and in-app channels. Their architecture uses a centralized notification aggregation service that deduplicates similar notifications ("John and 5 others liked your post" instead of 6 separate alerts), respects user-defined quiet hours, and employs machine learning to determine the optimal channel and timing for each notification. Their system processes billions of notifications daily with a priority queue architecture where security-critical alerts (login from new device) bypass all rate limits and are delivered within seconds, while social notifications are batched and optimized for engagement without causing fatigue.

Key Points & Summary

Multi-Channel Delivery

A notification system must support multiple delivery channels: push notifications via APNs (iOS) and FCM (Android), email via SMTP/SendGrid/SES, SMS via Twilio/SNS, in-app via WebSocket, and webhooks for third-party integrations. Each channel has unique constraints — SMS has 160-character limits, push requires device tokens, email needs HTML rendering. Channel selection is based on notification type, user preferences, and fallback logic.

Priority & Rate Limiting

Notifications are routed through priority-based queues: critical (security alerts, OTPs), high (order updates), medium (social interactions), and low (marketing). Rate limiting operates at multiple levels: per-user limits prevent fatigue (max 5 push/hour), per-channel limits respect provider quotas (SMS gateway throughput), and global limits protect the system during traffic spikes. Critical notifications bypass rate limits entirely.

Reliability & Retry Logic

Delivery failures are inevitable (device offline, email bounces, SMS gateway down). The system implements exponential backoff retries with jitter: 1st retry after 1 minute, 2nd after 5 minutes, 3rd after 30 minutes, up to a maximum retry count. Dead letter queues capture permanently failed notifications for manual review. Idempotency keys prevent duplicate deliveries on retry.

User Preferences & Opt-Out

Users control which notifications they receive, through which channels, and when. Preferences are stored per user and per notification category (marketing, transactional, security). Quiet hours suppress non-critical notifications during configured times. One-click unsubscribe links in emails are legally required (CAN-SPAM, GDPR). The preference service is checked before every notification dispatch.

Quick Summary

  • Support multiple channels: push (APNs/FCM), email (SMTP/SES), SMS (Twilio), in-app, webhooks
  • Priority queues ensure critical alerts are delivered within seconds regardless of system load
  • Rate limiting at per-user, per-channel, and global levels prevents notification fatigue
  • Exponential backoff retries with dead letter queues handle delivery failures gracefully
  • Template engine with i18n support renders personalized messages at scale
  • User preference service enforces opt-outs, quiet hours, and channel preferences before every send

Interview Questions & Answers

How would you design a notification system that supports multiple channels?

A multi-channel notification system requires a layered architecture:

  • Notification API: A unified ingestion endpoint that accepts notification requests with recipient, channel preferences, priority, template ID, and payload data. Validates input and publishes to a message queue.
  • Channel Router: Determines which channels to use based on: (1) notification type (OTPs require SMS), (2) user preferences (opted out of email), (3) device availability (no push token means skip push), and (4) fallback logic (push fails, try SMS).
  • Channel Workers: Dedicated worker pools per channel, each integrating with the appropriate provider API: APNs for iOS push, FCM for Android push, Amazon SES for email, Twilio for SMS. Each worker handles provider-specific formatting and error handling.
  • Delivery Tracker: Records the status of each notification attempt (queued, sent, delivered, opened, failed) in a database. Failed deliveries trigger retry logic or fallback to alternative channels.

How do you prevent notification fatigue while ensuring important alerts get through?

Balancing notification volume requires multiple strategies:

  • Priority Classification: Categorize every notification type into priority tiers. Critical (security, OTP) bypass all limits. High (transactional) have generous limits. Medium/Low (social, marketing) have strict limits and can be batched.
  • Per-User Rate Limiting: Implement a token bucket or sliding window counter per user per channel. Example: max 5 push notifications per hour, 10 emails per day, 2 SMS per day.
  • Notification Aggregation: Instead of sending 10 separate "Someone liked your post" notifications, aggregate them into "John and 9 others liked your post." This reduces volume by 90% for viral content.
  • Quiet Hours: Suppress non-critical notifications during user-configured quiet hours (e.g., 10 PM to 8 AM). Queue suppressed notifications and deliver them when quiet hours end.
  • Smart Delivery: Use machine learning to learn each user's engagement patterns and deliver notifications at optimal times for maximum open rates.

How do you handle notification delivery failures and retries?

Robust failure handling is critical for notification reliability:

  • Exponential Backoff: On failure, retry with increasing delays: 1 min, 5 min, 30 min, 2 hours, 24 hours. Add random jitter to prevent thundering herd when a provider recovers from an outage.
  • Channel Fallback: If the primary channel fails after max retries, fall back to the next preferred channel. Push notification failed? Try in-app. Email bounced? Try SMS for critical notifications.
  • Dead Letter Queue: Notifications that exhaust all retries across all channels are moved to a dead letter queue for manual investigation. Alerts fire when DLQ depth exceeds thresholds.
  • Idempotency: Each notification has a unique ID. Channel workers check for the ID before sending to prevent duplicate deliveries when retries overlap with delayed original deliveries.
  • Circuit Breaker: If a provider (e.g., Twilio) returns errors above a threshold, trip a circuit breaker to stop sending traffic. This prevents wasting resources and allows the provider to recover.

How would you implement user notification preferences at scale?

User preferences must be fast to query and flexible to configure:

  • Preference Schema: Store preferences as a matrix of (user_id, notification_category, channel) with values: enabled, disabled, or digest. Categories include security, transactional, social, marketing, and system.
  • Default Preferences: New users inherit sensible defaults (all transactional enabled, marketing off). Users override only what they want to change, keeping the storage sparse.
  • Caching: Cache user preferences in Redis with a TTL of 5-10 minutes. The notification worker checks preferences before every send. Cache invalidation is triggered on preference updates via a pub/sub event.
  • Legal Compliance: CAN-SPAM requires one-click unsubscribe in every marketing email. GDPR requires explicit opt-in for marketing communications. The preference service enforces these rules automatically, rejecting send requests that violate compliance.
  • Global Unsubscribe: Support a "mute all non-critical" toggle for users who want temporary silence without losing their granular preferences.
