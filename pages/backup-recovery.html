Backup & Recovery

Data protection and disaster recovery strategies

Intermediate ⏱ 25 min

Definition

What is Backup & Recovery?

Backup and recovery involves creating copies of data and systems to protect against data loss and enable restoration after failures or disasters.

Key Insight: Regular backups and tested recovery procedures are essential for business continuity.

Simple Explanation

Understanding the Concept

Think of backup and recovery like an insurance policy for your data. Just as you keep copies of important documents in a safe or a safety deposit box, backup systems create copies of digital data so it can be restored if something goes wrong.

How Backup & Recovery Works

1. Backup Strategies

  • Full Backup: A complete copy of all data, providing the simplest restoration but requiring the most storage and time
  • Incremental Backup: Copies only data changed since the last backup of any type, saving storage but requiring multiple backups for restoration
  • Differential Backup: Copies all data changed since the last full backup, balancing storage efficiency and recovery speed
  • Continuous Data Protection (CDP): Real-time replication that captures every change as it occurs

2. Key Metrics

  • RPO (Recovery Point Objective): Maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time
  • RTO (Recovery Time Objective): Maximum acceptable downtime before recovery completes
  • Retention Period: How long backup copies are kept before deletion
  • Backup Window: The time allocated for backup operations to complete

The 3-2-1 Backup Rule

3 Copies: Keep at least three copies of your data

2 Media Types: Store backups on at least two different media types

1 Off-site: Keep at least one copy in an off-site location

Real-World Example

AWS Backup Service

AWS Backup provides a centralized service for managing backups across AWS resources. It supports automated backup scheduling, lifecycle policies, and cross-region replication to ensure data durability and compliance.

Key Points & Summary

RPO & RTO

Recovery Point Objective defines max data loss tolerance; Recovery Time Objective defines max acceptable downtime.

Backup Types

Full, incremental, and differential backups each offer different trade-offs between storage, speed, and recovery complexity.

Disaster Recovery

DR plans ensure business continuity with strategies like hot, warm, and cold standby sites.

Testing

Regularly test recovery procedures to ensure backups are valid and restoration meets RTO/RPO targets.

Quick Summary

  • Backups protect against data loss from failures, disasters, and human error
  • RPO and RTO define acceptable data loss and downtime thresholds
  • The 3-2-1 rule provides a proven backup framework
  • Hot backups allow operations to continue during backup
  • Regular testing is critical to validate recovery procedures

Interview Questions & Answers

What are the different backup strategies?

Backup strategies include full backups (complete data copy), incremental backups (changes since last backup), differential backups (changes since last full backup), and continuous data protection with real-time replication.

What's the 3-2-1 backup rule?

The 3-2-1 rule states: keep 3 copies of data, on 2 different media types, with 1 copy off-site. This provides protection against various failure scenarios including hardware failure, natural disasters, and human error.

What is the difference between RPO and RTO?

RPO and RTO are critical disaster recovery metrics:

  • RPO (Recovery Point Objective): Maximum acceptable data loss, measured in time (e.g., 1 hour RPO means you can lose up to 1 hour of data)
  • RTO (Recovery Time Objective): Maximum acceptable downtime before system recovery (e.g., 4 hour RTO means the system must be back within 4 hours)
  • Trade-offs: Lower RPO/RTO requires more expensive infrastructure and more frequent backups
  • Business Impact: These metrics are driven by business requirements and compliance needs

What are hot, warm, and cold disaster recovery sites?

Disaster recovery sites differ in readiness and cost:

  • Hot Site: Fully operational duplicate infrastructure with real-time data replication; instant failover but highest cost
  • Warm Site: Pre-configured hardware with periodic data updates; recovery in hours at moderate cost
  • Cold Site: Basic infrastructure requiring setup and data restoration; recovery in days but lowest cost
  • Cloud DR: Cloud-based recovery using services like AWS, Azure, or GCP for flexible, pay-as-you-go disaster recovery
