Circuit Breaker Pattern

Prevent cascading failures in distributed systems by detecting faults and stopping repeated calls to failing services

Definition

What is the Circuit Breaker Pattern?

The Circuit Breaker pattern is a fault-tolerance design pattern used in distributed systems to detect failures and prevent an application from repeatedly trying to execute an operation that is likely to fail. Named after the electrical circuit breaker that automatically stops the flow of electricity when a fault is detected, this pattern wraps calls to external services in a circuit breaker object that monitors for failures. When failures reach a configured threshold, the circuit breaker trips and all subsequent calls return an error immediately without attempting to make the actual call, giving the failing service time to recover.

Key Insight: Just like an electrical circuit breaker protects your home from electrical fires by cutting power when it detects a dangerous surge, the software circuit breaker protects your system from cascading failures by cutting off calls to a struggling downstream service.

Simple Explanation

Understanding the Concept

Imagine you call a restaurant to place an order. If the phone rings busy once, you try again. But if you call 10 times in a row and it's always busy, you stop calling for a while and try again later. That's exactly what the circuit breaker pattern does for service-to-service communication.

The circuit breaker operates in three distinct states:

  • Closed (Normal): Requests flow through normally. The circuit breaker counts failures. If failures stay below the threshold, everything continues as usual. This is the healthy operating state.
  • Open (Tripped): When failures exceed the threshold, the circuit breaker trips open. All requests are immediately rejected with a fallback response, without even attempting to reach the downstream service. This prevents wasting resources on calls that will likely fail.
  • Half-Open (Testing): After a configured timeout period, the circuit breaker enters a half-open state. It allows a limited number of test requests through to the downstream service. If these succeed, the circuit breaker resets to closed. If they fail, it returns to open.
  • Failure Threshold: A configurable number of consecutive failures or failure percentage that triggers the circuit breaker to open. Common thresholds are 5 consecutive failures or a 50% failure rate.
  • Recovery Timeout: The duration the circuit breaker stays open before transitioning to half-open. This gives the failing service time to recover before being tested again.

Key benefits of the circuit breaker pattern include:

  • Fail Fast: Instead of waiting for timeouts, requests fail immediately when the circuit is open, preserving system resources
  • Graceful Degradation: The system can serve fallback responses instead of errors, maintaining partial functionality
  • Self-Healing: The half-open state allows automatic recovery when the downstream service comes back online
  • Prevent Cascading Failures: One failing service won't bring down the entire system by exhausting thread pools and connection limits

Real-World Example

Netflix Hystrix

Netflix developed Hystrix, one of the most well-known circuit breaker implementations, to handle the massive scale of their microservices architecture. With hundreds of microservices, a single slow or failing service could cascade and take down the entire platform. Hystrix wraps each service call in a circuit breaker, providing fallback behaviors like showing cached recommendations when the recommendation service is down, or displaying a generic movie list instead of personalized content. This ensures that even when individual services fail, Netflix continues to function for its millions of users.

Key Points & Summary

Three States

The circuit breaker operates in Closed (normal flow), Open (requests blocked), and Half-Open (testing recovery) states. Transitions between states are driven by failure counts, thresholds, and timeout durations configured per service.

Failure Threshold

Configure failure thresholds based on consecutive failures, failure rate percentage, or both. A common configuration is tripping after 5 failures in a 60-second window or when the failure rate exceeds 50% across recent requests.

Timeout & Recovery

The recovery timeout determines how long the circuit stays open before attempting recovery. Too short and you overwhelm a recovering service; too long and you delay recovery. Typical values range from 10 to 60 seconds.

Fallback Mechanisms

When the circuit is open, provide meaningful fallback responses: cached data, default values, alternative service calls, or graceful error messages. Fallbacks ensure users still get a usable experience during partial outages.

Quick Summary

  • Circuit breaker prevents cascading failures by stopping calls to failing services
  • Three states: Closed (normal), Open (blocking), Half-Open (testing)
  • Failure thresholds trigger the transition from closed to open state
  • Recovery timeouts allow the failing service time to heal before retrying
  • Fallback mechanisms provide graceful degradation during outages

Interview Questions & Answers

What is the Circuit Breaker pattern and why is it important in microservices?

The Circuit Breaker pattern is a fault-tolerance mechanism that prevents cascading failures in distributed systems. It wraps service calls and monitors for failures. When failures exceed a threshold, it "trips" and immediately returns fallback responses without attempting the call. It's crucial in microservices because:

  • A single failing service can exhaust thread pools and connections in upstream services
  • Without circuit breakers, failures cascade across the entire service mesh
  • It provides fail-fast behavior, freeing resources for requests that can succeed
  • It enables graceful degradation instead of total system failure
  • It gives failing services time to recover without being overwhelmed by retries

Explain the three states of a circuit breaker and their transitions.

The three states and their transitions are:

  • Closed: The default state where all requests pass through. Failures are counted. When the failure count or rate exceeds the configured threshold within a time window, the circuit transitions to Open.
  • Open: All requests are immediately rejected with a fallback response. No calls are made to the downstream service. After a configured timeout period (e.g., 30 seconds), the circuit transitions to Half-Open.
  • Half-Open: A limited number of test requests are allowed through. If these succeed, the circuit transitions back to Closed (recovery confirmed). If any fail, the circuit transitions back to Open and the timeout resets.

The key configuration parameters are: failure threshold (count or percentage), time window for counting failures, recovery timeout duration, and the number of test requests allowed in half-open state.

How does the Circuit Breaker pattern differ from simple retry logic?

While both handle failures, they serve different purposes and work best together:

  • Retry Logic: Attempts the same request multiple times, assuming the failure is transient. Good for intermittent network issues but can overwhelm a struggling service with repeated attempts.
  • Circuit Breaker: Stops all requests after detecting a pattern of failures. Assumes the service is down and needs time to recover. Prevents resource waste and cascading failures.
  • Combined Approach: Use retries for individual transient failures (with exponential backoff), and wrap them in a circuit breaker that trips when retries consistently fail across multiple requests.

The circuit breaker adds system-level awareness, while retries handle request-level transient errors.

What are effective fallback strategies when a circuit breaker is open?

Effective fallback strategies include:

  • Cached Data: Return the last known good response from a local cache. Works well for data that doesn't change frequently, like product catalogs or user profiles.
  • Default Values: Return sensible default responses. For example, show a generic recommendation list when the personalization service is down.
  • Alternative Service: Route the request to a backup or secondary service that provides similar functionality, potentially with reduced features.
  • Queue for Later: Accept the request, queue it for processing when the service recovers, and acknowledge receipt to the user.
  • Graceful Error Message: Return a user-friendly message explaining limited functionality, rather than a raw error or timeout.
